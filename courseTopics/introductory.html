Pips and Ticks

A pip is a unit of measurment in the market. If you're trading a currency. Let's use AUD/USD for example. This is the Australian Dollar versus the US Dollar. The numbers will look a little different. But, it's currently valued at $0.66536. A pip is the increment that price moves. One full pip in this case is price going from $0.66536 to $0.66546. One small movement.

A tick is very similar to a pip, but instead it relates to when you're trading futures. We'll use Nasdaq for this example. It's currently at $18,570.75. The tick is measured here in quarters. So for every quarter that price moves, you move one tick. If price went from $18,570.75 to $18,571.75, you moved a whole dollar. Being four quarters, you moved 4 ticks.

Trading indices or currencies also allows you to place a particular dollar amount on how much you want to value each pip or tick. This is important because it allows you to make way more money off the movements rather than normal stocks.

Futures have set tick values. You choose whether or not you're okay with the tick value each item you decide you want to trade.

Forex, you have lot sizes. No matter what the size of your account may be, you can always trade the smallest lot size. Which is normally $0.10 a pip. Which means you can have a 10 pip move and make $1.00 or you can have a 10 pip move and make $1,000.00. It all depends on your lot size.

Leverage

The final topic we're going to discuss is Leverage. Leverage plays a critical role in your trading. Leverage is what's going to make the difference in how big of a lot size you can use.

Let's break it down some more. If you have an account with $10.00 in it and you're using a broker that offers a 1:50 leverage, that means that you can technically trade as if you had a $500.00 account balance instead. Most regulated brokers have a 1:50 leverage. Unregulated on the other hand normally has a 1:500 leverage. If you go that route, your $10.00 account would be able to trade as if you had $5,000.00 in your account instead.

We'll dive deeper into unregulated and regulated brokers in a later section.
